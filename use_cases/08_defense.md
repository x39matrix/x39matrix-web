## THE CURRENT STATE OF GOVERNMENT DIGITAL INFRASTRUCTURE

Government digital infrastructure is centralized, outdated, and vulnerable. The consequences are measured not in dollars but in national security:

- SolarWinds Attack (2020): 18,000+ organizations compromised including US Treasury, Department of Homeland Security, and the Pentagon. Attackers had access for 9 months before detection.
- Colonial Pipeline (2021): Ransomware shut down the largest fuel pipeline in the US for 6 days. Paid $4.4 million ransom.
- Microsoft Exchange (2021): Chinese state-sponsored hackers exploited vulnerabilities affecting 250,000+ servers worldwide including government agencies.
- MOVEit (2023): Supply chain attack exposed data from hundreds of government agencies and corporations globally.

Global cyber defense spending exceeds $150 billion annually. Most of this money goes to reactive systems: firewalls, intrusion detection, incident response. The infrastructure itself remains fundamentally vulnerable because it is centralized. One compromised server, one stolen credential, one supply chain attack can cascade through entire government networks.

Military communications rely on classified proprietary systems (SIPRNet, JWICS) that are expensive to maintain, slow to update, and cannot interoperate easily with allied forces. The defense industrial base has a 4-year average procurement cycle. By the time a system is deployed, it is already outdated.
